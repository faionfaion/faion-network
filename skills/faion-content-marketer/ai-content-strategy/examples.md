# AI Content Strategy - Examples

## Example 1: SaaS Content Stack

**Company:** Project management tool (competing with Asana, Monday.com)

**Generic AI Content (DON'T DO):**
```
"Project management is important for teams. It helps organize tasks
and improve collaboration. Using tools like Asana, Monday.com, and
Notion can help manage projects effectively."
```

**Differentiated AI + Human Content (DO THIS):**
```
"Why remote teams should ditch spreadsheet-based project tracking
(and what we learned migrating 200+ teams)

At [Company], we watched teams waste 8+ hours/week on project updates
in spreadsheets. Here's what we discovered...

[First-hand case study: Team A's process]
[Proprietary data: Our anonymized dataset of 1,000 teams]
[Expert quote from industry researcher]
[Our contrarian take: Why most project tools fail]
[Real numbers: Time saved by migrating]
```

**AI Role:** Generate structure, research competitor approaches, optimize SEO
**Human Role:** Add case studies, insert company data, share experiences, take positions

## Example 2: Content Marketing Topic

**Topic:** Email newsletter growth strategy

**Generic Version:**
```
"Email newsletters are important for building an audience.
You should write good content, send regularly, and grow your list.
Here are some best practices for growing your email list."

1. Lead magnets (everyone does this)

2. Content + community (our breakthrough)
   - Result: 12% signup rate

   - Lessons: [What surprised us]

3. Paid amplification strategy (controversial take)
   - Why newsletters are the new SEO
   - Cost per subscriber analysis
   - ROI vs. social media

[Real metrics, screenshots, anonymized data]
[Interview with 3 newsletter creators]
[What didn't work and why]
```

3. [Factor 3 - backed by case studies]

## Example 4: AI Prompt for Content Creation

**Good Prompt (Provides Context):**
```
You are writing for marketing leaders at B2B SaaS companies (50-500 employees).
They're frustrated with AI content that sounds generic and doesn't drive results.

Topic: How to use AI for content without sounding generic

Your angle: Focus on the proprietary data + human expertise combination
Target audience comprehension: 6th-8th grade reading level

Structure:
1. Problem: Why most AI content fails (common mistake we see)
2. The shift: From "prompt and publish" to "AI-assisted creation"
3. Our approach: Specific workflow with real examples
4. Results: Metrics showing differentiation works
5. Next steps: How to implement

Tone: Helpful, specific, opinionated
Include: 1 specific example, 1 data point, 1 expert opinion
Avoid: Generic advice, hypotheticals, industry jargon

Output: 800 words in markdown
```

**Bad Prompt (Vague):**
```
Write about AI content.
Make it helpful and specific.
Include examples.
```

## Example 5: Transformation - Weak to Strong

**Stage 1: Initial AI Draft**
```
"AI content is useful for businesses. It can save time and create more content.
Using ChatGPT or other AI tools can help with brainstorming and drafting."
```

**Stage 2: Human Add First-Hand Experience**
```
"When we started using AI for content in 2023, we thought we could just
'prompt and publish.' That failed immediately. Here's what actually worked:

We tested AI across 50+ pieces. The winners all had one thing in common:
human expertise added proprietary insights the AI couldn't generate."
```

**Stage 3: Add Proprietary Data**
```
"When we started using AI for content in 2023, we tested it across 50+ pieces.
Result: AI-only content ranked poorly. But AI + human expertise? Different story.

Our data:
- AI only: 2% CTR, 15% rankings drop within 6 months
- AI + human insights: 7% CTR, 35% improvement in rankings
- Differentiator: Articles with proprietary data ranked 3x better

Why? Google's E-E-A-T update rewards experience and original research."
```

**Stage 4: Add Expert Voice & Opinion**
```
"When we started using AI for content in 2023, we tested it across 50+ pieces.
AI-only content ranked poorly. But AI + human expertise? Different story.

Our data shows a clear 3x ranking advantage for differentiated content.
But here's what surprised us: The best-performing pieces weren't the longest
or most comprehensive. They were the ones with specific opinions.

Example: Instead of 'here are 10 tips,' we wrote 'why tip #5 is a waste of time'
(backed by our data). That piece gets 2x the engagement.

My take: The next wave of AI content will be ruthlessly specific,
heavily opinionated, and deeply experiential. Generic is dead."
```

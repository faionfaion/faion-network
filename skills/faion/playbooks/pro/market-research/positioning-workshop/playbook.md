## Goal

After this playbook you will have a 1-page positioning doc — signed off by your team — that names your competitive frame, your differentiating attributes, the value those attributes deliver, your best-fit customer profile, and a tight "X for Y who want Z" positioning statement. You will also have a documented rationale for every choice, so the next hire or client can understand why you positioned the product this way.

## Prerequisites

- Completed the `positioning-basics` playbook (free tier) or equivalent solo exercise. This workshop extends that work into a facilitated team format.
- 3–6 participants: at minimum one founder/owner, one person who talks to customers daily (sales, CS, or support), and one senior delivery person (developer, designer, or strategist). External contractors welcome — they often surface blind spots.
- Raw material collected before the session: a list of at least 5 competitive alternatives (real products or substitute behaviours), at least 10 customer interview notes or support threads, and any existing positioning copy (landing page hero, pitch deck slide 1, onboarding email).
- A shared screen + whiteboard tool (Miro, FigJam, or a physical whiteboard with a camera for remote participants). Prepare a blank board with six labelled zones before the session starts: _Competitive Alternatives_, _Unique Attributes_, _Value Translation_, _Market Category Options_, _Statement Draft_, _Validation_.
- One facilitator assigned in advance. The facilitator runs the clock, enforces the rules ("no feature lists in the value zone"), and writes the final doc. They should not also be the loudest voice on product direction.
- 30 minutes of async prep: each participant reads the existing competitive-alternatives list and adds at least one entry before arriving.

## Steps

1. **Open the session and set the rules (5 min).** Facilitator reads these three rules aloud: (a) Every claim must reference a real alternative — "we're better" is not allowed without naming better than what. (b) Features belong in the Attributes zone, outcomes belong in the Value zone — do not mix them. (c) All votes are equal; seniority does not override dot-voting results. Start a shared timer visible to the room.

2. **Round 1 — Competitive alternatives (30 min).** Each participant silently adds sticky notes to the _Competitive Alternatives_ zone for 10 minutes: one alternative per note, named specifically (e.g. "HubSpot free CRM", "a shared Airtable base", "hiring a part-time ops coordinator"). No discussion yet. After 10 minutes, facilitator reads each note aloud. Cluster duplicates. Dot-vote: each participant has 3 votes. Keep the top 5–7 alternatives by vote count. Discard the rest. Write the surviving list in a shared doc under the heading `Competitive alternatives` — this is column 1 of your positioning doc.

3. **Round 2 — Unique-attributes voting (30 min).** Each participant silently adds attributes to the _Unique Attributes_ zone for 10 minutes: one attribute per note, phrased as "Unlike [alternative from Round 1], we…". Facilitator strictly removes any note that does not start with "Unlike". After 10 minutes, cluster and dot-vote with 5 votes each. Keep the top 4–6 attributes. For each surviving attribute, the facilitator asks: "Does any competitor also do this?" If yes and no rebuttal surfaces in 60 seconds, discard the attribute. Document survivors under `Unique attributes`.

4. **Round 3 — Value translation (30 min).** For each unique attribute from Round 2, the team collectively writes the customer outcome: what changes in the customer's work or life because this attribute exists? Use the format "This means [customer role] can [outcome] instead of [pain/status quo]." Example: attribute = "auto-generated weekly digest for clients" → value = "This means agency owners can send a professional client update in 2 minutes instead of spending 45 minutes compiling screenshots." Write one value statement per attribute. This is column 3 of your positioning doc. Flag any attribute where the team cannot agree on a value statement — that attribute may not be differentiated enough to keep.

5. **Round 4 — Market-category options (30 min).** Facilitator writes three market-category candidates on the board derived from the value statements: (a) the obvious category your product already occupies, (b) a narrower niche category that emphasises your strongest differentiator, (c) a new category you could credibly create or occupy first. Team discusses the strategic tradeoffs for each: what buyers does each category attract? what competitors does it invite? what price expectation does it set? Dot-vote with 3 votes each to select one category. Document the chosen category and the runner-up under `Market category` with a one-sentence rationale for the choice.

6. **Round 5 — "X for Y who want Z" iteration (30 min).** Facilitator drafts three positioning-statement candidates on the board using the formula: `[product name] is [market category] for [customer profile] who want [primary value outcome]`. Use the outputs from Rounds 2–5 as raw material. Each candidate must use a different customer profile (job title + company type or life situation) to test scope. Example: "Clientflow is project-tracking software for independent web studios who want to close projects without chasing client approvals." Team silent-reads all three for 3 minutes, then each participant marks the one that feels most true AND most useful to a cold prospect. Discuss the top vote-getter: does it exclude the wrong people? Does the value clause match the strongest outcome from Round 3? Rewrite until the room reaches rough consensus (no one strongly objects). Document the draft statement.

7. **Round 6 — Positioning statement validation (30 min).** Run the following three checks before closing the session. (a) **Cold-read test:** one participant who spoke least reads the statement aloud as if for the first time and answers: "What kind of company is this? Who is it for? Why should I care?" If any answer is wrong or vague, revise. (b) **Competitive filter test:** apply the statement to each alternative from Round 1 — if the statement also describes a competitor's product, it is not differentiated enough. Identify the gap and revise. (c) **Sales-script test:** the person who talks to customers most imagines using this statement as the first sentence in a cold email or intro call. Would a target customer keep reading? If not, adjust the value clause. After all three checks pass, facilitator writes the final 1-page positioning doc combining all six columns. Distribute to all participants before the meeting ends.

## Verify

Open the finished 1-page positioning doc and confirm all six fields are filled:

```
Competitive alternatives: [5–7 named alternatives]
Unique attributes:        [4–6 "Unlike X, we…" statements]
Value delivered:          [one outcome per attribute]
Best-fit customer:        [job title + company type or life situation]
Market category:          [chosen frame + one-sentence rationale]
Positioning statement:    "[product] is [category] for [customer] who want [value]"
```

Then run the cold-read test with one person who was not in the workshop. Ask them to read only the positioning statement and answer: "What does this product do, who is it for, and why should they care?" All three answers must be correct without clarification.

## Troubleshooting

| Symptom | Cause | Fix |
|---------|-------|-----|
| Round 2 produces 15+ surviving attributes after voting | Team is listing product features, not differentiators | Re-read the "Unlike [X], we…" rule; run a second cull pass asking "does HubSpot/Notion/any large platform also do this?" — if yes and no rebuttal, cut it |
| No agreement on market category in Round 4 | Founder and sales rep have different target customers in mind | Surface the disagreement explicitly; run the ICP vote first (who is the best-fit customer?) and let category follow from customer — do not force consensus on category before ICP is clear |
| Positioning statement sounds like every SaaS tagline ("simple, powerful, flexible") | Value clause uses adjectives, not outcomes | Replace adjectives with measurable or observable customer behaviours: "reclaim 3 hours per week", "close deals without a second meeting", "ship a client site in 4 weeks" |
| Cold-read test fails even after two revisions | Market category is unfamiliar to the target buyer | Try the obvious/established category from Round 4's option (a) — new categories require education budgets most studios do not have |
| One senior participant dominates and kills ideas before voting | Psychological safety is low | Facilitator enforces the silent-write phases strictly; if the senior person speaks during silent phases, pause the clock and reset |
| Team finishes Round 5 but no statement wins majority | Customer profiles are too different across the three drafts | Go back to the value-translation outputs and identify which customer role appears in the most value statements — that is the primary ICP; write all three statement candidates for that single profile |

## Next

- Run the output through `competitor-analysis` to stress-test competitive alternatives — the Round 1 list may be missing indirect substitutes your customers actually consider.
- Share the 1-page positioning doc with 3 existing customers within one week: ask each one "does this describe why you use us?" and record verbatim reactions. Revisions triggered by ≥2 similar objections are worth the rework.
- Revisit this workshop every 6 months or after any significant product pivot, new ICP hypothesis, or entry of a well-funded competitor into your category.

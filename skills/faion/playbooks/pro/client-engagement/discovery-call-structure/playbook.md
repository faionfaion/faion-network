## Goal

After this playbook you will have a structured 30-minute discovery call framework — including a timed agenda, scripted questions for each phase, and a clear next-step close — that surfaces the client's actual problem before you mention any solution.

## Prerequisites

- A scheduled call with a prospective client (Google Meet, Zoom, or phone — medium does not matter).
- A shared notes doc open during the call (Notion page, Google Doc, or Apple Notes — anything you can scroll back after).
- Familiarity with the client's domain: spend 10 minutes on their website and LinkedIn profile before the call.
- Prior playbook: `statement-of-work` (linked when you need to convert discovery findings into a formal engagement).

## Steps

1. **Send a 24-hour reminder with a one-line agenda.** Email or calendar note: "30 min. Goal: understand your situation. I'll ask questions first, then we can discuss fit." Sets expectations — client arrives ready to talk, not to listen.

2. **Open with rapport (minutes 0–5).** Start with a specific observation, not a generic opener:
   - "I saw you launched your checkout redesign last month — how did that roll out?"
   - "Your job post mentioned you're scaling from 3 to 12 engineers — what triggered that decision?"
   Avoid: "Tell me about your business." (too broad, burns rapport time on irrelevant backstory.)

3. **Diagnose the problem (minutes 5–20).** Work through three core questions in order. Do not skip ahead.

   **Question 1 — What's broken?**
   - "Walk me through what's not working right now. Concretely — what did you try to do last week that failed or took longer than it should?"
   - Follow-up: "How long has this been going on?"
   - Follow-up: "Who else on your team feels this most?"

   **Question 2 — What have you already tried?**
   - "Before reaching out to me, what did you try to fix this? Hired someone? Used a tool? Did it yourselves?"
   - Follow-up: "What worked partially, and where did it break down?"
   - This tells you: (a) their sophistication level, (b) solutions already ruled out, (c) their real budget ceiling (revealed by what they already paid for).

   **Question 3 — What does success look like?**
   - "If we solve this perfectly — what changes in your day-to-day six months from now? What would you be able to do that you can't do today?"
   - Follow-up: "Is there a number attached to that? Revenue, time saved, team headcount?"
   - This anchors your future proposal to their language, not yours.

   **Active listening rules during this phase:**
   - Take verbatim notes on pain phrases — you will echo these back in your proposal.
   - Do not suggest solutions. If you catch yourself saying "we could…" or "one approach is…" — stop. Ask another question instead.
   - If the client asks "what would you do?" respond: "Great question — I want to make sure I understand the full picture first. Can I ask one more thing?"

4. **Reality-check budget and timeline (minutes 20–25).**

   Ask both questions directly. Indirect versions waste everyone's time.

   **Budget:**
   - "Do you have a budget range in mind for this? Even a rough one helps me understand what's realistic."
   - If they deflect ("it depends on the proposal"): "Totally fair. To calibrate — is this a €5k engagement, a €50k one, or something in between?"
   - If the number is too low for the problem they described, name it now: "Based on what you've told me, a proper solution runs €X–Y. Does that fit, or do we need to scope something smaller?"

   **Timeline:**
   - "When does this need to be solved? Is there a deadline driving this — a launch, a board meeting, a contract expiry?"
   - A hard deadline is a buying signal. No deadline often means no urgency — probe: "What happens if this isn't solved in six months?"

5. **Close with a single concrete next step (minutes 25–30).**

   Never end with "I'll send something over." Name the artifact and the date:
   - "Based on what you've told me, I'll put together a one-page outline of how I'd approach this, along with a rough cost range. I can have that to you by Thursday. Does that work?"
   - If you need a second call: "I'd like to bring in my colleague who handles [X] — can we do a 45-minute working session next Tuesday?"
   - If it's clearly not a fit: "Honestly, what you need is more of a [X] than a [Y] — I'm not the right fit, but I can point you to someone who is." (Referral builds more goodwill than a bad project.)

6. **Send the follow-up within 2 hours.** Three-paragraph email:
   - Paragraph 1: recap their pain in their words ("You mentioned that X is costing you Y every week…").
   - Paragraph 2: one-line on what you're going to send and when.
   - Paragraph 3: confirm the next step with a calendar link or explicit date.

## Verify

Check your notes immediately after the call. You should be able to answer all four without looking up the client's website:

1. What specific thing is broken in their workflow right now?
2. What have they already tried, and why did it fail?
3. What does success look like in their words — ideally with a metric?
4. What is their budget range and deadline?

If you cannot answer any of these four, the discovery was incomplete. Book a 15-minute follow-up call to fill the gap before writing a proposal.

## Troubleshooting

| Symptom | Cause | Fix |
|---------|-------|-----|
| Client talks for 20 minutes without a concrete problem surfacing | They rehearsed a pitch for you instead of sharing a problem | Interrupt politely: "That context is really helpful — can I ask a more specific question? Last week, what task took longer than it should have?" |
| Client refuses to name a budget | Fear of anchoring high; or no internal approval yet | Offer a range: "Is this closer to a €5k project or a €50k one?" — even a "neither" narrows the field |
| Client asks for a proposal before you have enough info | Impatience, or they're collecting quotes | "I want to make sure the proposal actually solves the right problem — give me 5 more minutes to ask a couple of questions." Most people agree. |
| Call runs over 30 minutes with no next step agreed | Discovery phase too long; problem is genuinely complex | Schedule a second discovery call explicitly scoped to budget/timeline; do not extend the current call |
| Client describes a problem that is out of your scope | Mismatch identified in discovery | Name it directly, offer a referral. Do not scope-fit a project you cannot execute well. |
| Client says "we just need someone to execute, we know what we want" | They may have skipped the problem definition internally | Ask: "What's the outcome you're trying to achieve with this execution?" — surface the Why behind the What |

## Goal

After this playbook you will have signed up at least 10 paying (or committed pilot) customers by working entirely without ads — sourcing them through warm introductions, hand-picked cold DMs, and manual onboarding calls — and you will have a conversion log that shows what worked and what to drop.

## Prerequisites

- A problem hypothesis confirmed by at least two customer interviews (see `mom-test-customer-interview`).
- A minimal working surface for customers to land on: a landing page, a Typeform, or even a calendly link for a discovery call. No full product required yet.
- A spreadsheet or plain text file ready to track 60–80 prospects (columns: Name, Channel, Contacted date, Reply Y/N, Call booked Y/N, Paid/Committed Y/N, Notes).
- Accounts on the networks where your target persona spends time: LinkedIn, Twitter/X, Reddit, or a relevant Discord server. Pick the one channel where the most promising 50 targets live.
- A one-sentence "why I'm reaching out" that is honest and specific to the recipient. Not a template — a real sentence.

## Steps

1. Build a list of 10 warm contacts: people who already know you (former colleagues, university contacts, Slack friends) and who match your target persona or can introduce you to someone who does. Write their names in the tracker under "Warm intro" channel. You do not need permission to add someone to a list — you need it before you send.

2. Message each warm contact directly with a personal note. Use your real messaging platform (iMessage, WhatsApp, LinkedIn DM, email — wherever you actually talk to them). Example message for a side-project targeting freelance designers: "Hey Marta — I'm building something for freelance designers who lose revision feedback across Slack and email. You work with a lot of designers. Would you be willing to either try it yourself or introduce me to one person who might? 15-minute call is all I need."

3. For every warm contact who declines or doesn't reply after 5 days, ask for one introduction instead: "No worries — do you know anyone who fits that profile? A quick intro would mean a lot."

4. Build a list of 50 cold targets on the network where your persona is most active. Concrete search methods by network:
   - **LinkedIn**: search job title "freelance designer" + filter "1st and 2nd connections" + "posted in last 30 days". Pick people who recently posted about client work or project management.
   - **Twitter/X**: search `"client feedback" lang:en -filter:links` or your equivalent. Read recent tweets. Add only people who described the problem in their own words.
   - **Reddit**: search `subreddit:freelance "revision" OR "client feedback"` and find active commenters — not just post authors.
   - **Discord**: join 2–3 relevant servers (e.g., Designer Hangout, Indie Hackers). Read channels like #show-work or #tools for 48 hours before DMing anyone.

5. Send cold DMs in batches of 10 per day. Write each DM from scratch — do not paste the same template. Each DM must do exactly three things in ≤4 sentences: reference something real you saw from them, state the problem in their language (not yours), and ask one yes/no question. Example: "I saw your post about juggling three client revision rounds at once — that looked painful. I'm building a tool that consolidates all feedback into one thread per project. Would a free 2-week pilot be worth 15 minutes of your time this week?" Do not attach links in the opening DM.

6. Track every reply in your spreadsheet. Reply rate benchmark: 15–25 % on cold DMs to hand-picked targets is realistic. If you are under 10 % after 20 DMs, rewrite the opening line — not the whole message, just the first sentence.

7. For every person who says "yes" or "maybe", book a 30-minute onboarding call immediately. Use https://cal.com (free) or https://calendly.com (free tier). Send the link in the same reply thread. Do not hand over a sign-up link first — get them on a call.

8. Run each onboarding call manually. Share your screen, walk them through the product or prototype step by step, answer every question live, and take notes. The goal of the call is not to demo — it is to get them to do the first action that constitutes "using" your product (submit a project, invite a team member, whatever the activation event is). Do not end the call until they have done that action or explicitly said they will not.

9. At the end of the onboarding call, ask directly: "Based on what you saw today, is this worth $[your price] per month to you?" If yes, collect payment before ending the call or the session. Use https://stripe.com/payments/payment-links (free to create) or request via PayPal/Wise. Do not offer "free access for now" unless it is a time-bounded pilot with a stated end date.

10. After every call — win or loss — write one line in your tracker: what they said yes to, what objection killed the deal, or what confused them. After 5 calls, read all the lines together. The pattern that emerges is your next product iteration.

11. Repeat until 10 paying or committed customers appear in your tracker. If you exhaust 60 prospects without reaching 10, revisit your problem hypothesis — the channel or the persona is off, not the outreach volume.

## Verify

Open your tracker spreadsheet. You have completed this playbook when:

- The "Paid/Committed" column shows at least 10 entries marked Y.
- Each of those 10 entries has a corresponding call date and at least one note.
- The tracker shows at least 60 total prospects contacted (to give the conversion math meaning).

If fewer than 10 of 60 converted, the playbook is not yet complete — but the tracker now contains the data to diagnose why. Read the "Notes" column, find the most common objection, and update your offer or persona before contacting the next batch.

## Troubleshooting

| Symptom | Cause | Fix |
|---------|-------|-----|
| Nobody replies to cold DMs | Opening line is generic or about your product, not them | Rewrite the first sentence to reference something specific they posted or said. Test: would this sentence make sense sent to 100 different people? If yes, it is too generic. |
| People reply but don't book a call | Asking too late or asking for too much commitment upfront | Send the cal.com link in the same reply as your confirmation: "Great — here's a 30-min slot: [link]. Tuesday or Wednesday works best?" One click, not a negotiation. |
| Calls booked but no-shows | Link was sent but no reminder was set | Enable cal.com email reminders (Settings → Notifications → 24h + 1h before). Also send a manual WhatsApp or Twitter DM the morning of the call. |
| Call went well but they didn't pay | Payment was left as a follow-up action | Ask for payment on the call, not after. Present the Stripe payment link screen-shared on the call: "If this makes sense for you, I can take a card right now and you're set." |
| Warm contacts ignore the ask | The request is unclear or too large | Reduce the ask to one introduction: "Do you know one person who would talk to me for 15 minutes?" is easier to act on than "Can you try this and give me feedback?" |
| Reply rate under 10 % after 30 DMs | Targeting is off — people don't recognise the problem in your words | Go back to Reddit or the Discord and read 20 more posts. Copy exact phrases people use to describe the pain. Use those phrases verbatim in your DM. |

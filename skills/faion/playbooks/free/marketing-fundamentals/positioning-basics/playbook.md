## Goal

After this playbook you will have a one-sentence positioning statement — "X for Y who want Z" — that makes your product's value immediately obvious to a cold stranger in your target segment. You will also have a short context document capturing the five inputs that produced the statement, so you can revisit it as the product evolves.

## Prerequisites

- At least five conversations with real users or prospects (interviews, sales calls, support threads).
- A list of competitors, alternatives, or substitutes your target buyers consider — even if that list is "spreadsheet" or "nothing, they do it manually".
- A rough sense of who your happiest potential users are: job title, company size, workflow, or any observable attribute that groups them.
- A text file or doc where you can accumulate notes during the exercise (Google Docs, Notion, or a plain `.md` file all work).

## Steps

1. Open your notes file and write the heading **Competitive alternatives** at the top. List every alternative a prospective customer would reach for if your product did not exist. Be concrete: name the specific product, workflow, or behaviour. Examples for a lightweight project-tracker aimed at freelancers: "Trello (free tier)", "a shared Google Doc", "paper notepad", "nothing — they email back and forth". Aim for at least three entries.

2. Under the heading **Unique attributes**, list every capability, characteristic, or constraint of your product that genuinely differs from the alternatives in Step 1. Do not list features you copied. Do not list things every product in the space does. For each entry, write one sentence starting with "Unlike [alternative], we…". Example: "Unlike Trello, we generate a client-facing PDF summary in one click." Aim for three to five attributes.

3. Under **Value delivered**, translate each attribute from Step 2 into a customer outcome. Ask yourself: "So what? Why does this matter to the buyer?" for every attribute. The attribute lives in your product; the value lives in the customer's life or job. Example translation: attribute = "one-click PDF summary" → value = "freelancers reclaim 30 minutes per project close and look more professional to clients". Write the value, not the feature.

4. Under **Who cares most**, identify the buyer segment for whom the values in Step 3 are most important — not most broadly appealing, but most urgently felt. Narrow is better: "EU-based freelance web designers billing by the project" is more actionable than "freelancers". Reread your customer conversations and look for the subset where the problem came up first, loudest, or most often. Write one tightly-scoped persona sentence: job role + context + pain trigger.

5. Under **Market category**, choose the one-to-three-word category frame that signals to the Step 4 persona what kind of product this is and what it replaces. The category primes expectations before a single feature is explained. Examples: "project tracker", "client portal", "invoicing tool". Avoid invented category names — use the words your persona already searches for, even if your product is better than every existing option in that category.

6. Draft your first positioning statement by combining steps 1–5 into the template:

   > **[Product name] is a [market category] for [who cares most] who want [top value from Step 3].**

   Example using the inputs above: "Delivra is a project tracker for EU-based freelance web designers who want client-ready PDF summaries without reformatting work."

7. Stress-test the draft with three questions, one at a time. Write your answer under each question in your notes file:
   - **Does the persona recognise the problem in Step 3 without explanation?** If you have to explain why the value matters, your persona description is too broad — narrow Step 4 further.
   - **Could a direct competitor claim the same statement truthfully?** If yes, your differentiator is missing — add the sharpest item from Step 2 into the statement.
   - **Does a cold stranger reading this know what to do next?** If not, the market category (Step 5) is wrong — swap it for a more recognisable one.

8. Rewrite the statement incorporating any fixes from Step 7. Repeat the stress test once. Stop after the second iteration — over-optimising at this stage produces corporate word-salad. Lock the statement and write it at the top of your notes file as **Final positioning statement v1.0**.

9. Apply the statement in three places right now to make it real: (a) the `<title>` tag and hero headline of your landing page; (b) the first line of your Twitter/X or LinkedIn bio; (c) the first sentence of your next cold DM or email. These three placements create immediate feedback — you will know within two weeks if the statement resonates.

## Verify

Open your landing page (or a staging preview) and read the hero headline as a first-time visitor. Run this check:

- The headline contains your market category, your target persona signal, and one clear outcome.
- You can read the headline aloud in under five seconds and a friend unfamiliar with your product can explain back what it does.
- The statement does not contain the words "seamlessly", "powerful", "all-in-one", "next-generation", or any other category-generic adjective.

If all three pass, the playbook is complete. If the friend test fails, return to Step 7 and identify which of the three stress-test questions your statement is failing.

## Troubleshooting

| Symptom | Cause | Fix |
|---------|-------|-----|
| Step 2 produces a list of generic features, not real differentiators | You compared yourself to an idealised competitor, not a real one | Go back to Step 1 and name the exact product version buyers are actually using today (Trello free tier, not "project management software"). Then redo Step 2 against that specific product. |
| The "who cares most" segment keeps expanding | Fear of narrowing too soon | Pick the five users from your conversations who paid, converted, or showed the strongest pain signal. Describe only those five people. Everyone else is a secondary segment for a future playbook. |
| Every market category you try feels wrong or undersells the product | You are inventing a new category | New categories require years of education budget most solo founders don't have. Pick the closest existing category your persona already searches for, add one qualifier ("async" + "standup tool"), and move on. You can rename the category after you have 1,000 customers. |
| The final statement sounds like every other SaaS tagline | You skipped the "could a competitor claim this?" check in Step 7 | Run the check now: paste your statement and replace your product name with a top competitor's name. If it still reads true, you have no differentiator in the statement. Pull the sharpest item from Step 2 — your most unique attribute — and embed it explicitly. |
| You have no competitive alternatives to list | You think your product has no competition | Your competition is the current behaviour: spreadsheets, paper, "I just don't do it", or a workaround in a tool built for something else. List what people do today to cope with the problem, not just named software products. |

# Business Storytelling

## Summary

Business storytelling structures information so the audience receives the conclusion first, supported by evidence — not the reverse. The core frameworks are Pyramid Principle (answer → arguments → evidence), SCQA (Situation → Complication → Question → Answer), and Pixar (narrative arc for case studies and change initiatives). Every claim must pass the "so what" test: a fact without an implication is incomplete.

## Why

People remember stories 22x better than facts alone. Audiences disengage when they must read through background to reach the conclusion. Leading with the answer lets busy readers stop when satisfied; the supporting structure provides depth for those who continue. Data storytelling fails when metrics are presented without implications — "99.9% uptime" means nothing until translated to "fewer than 9 hours of downtime per year."

## When To Use

- Drafting executive summaries, board updates, or investor pitches that must lead with a recommendation
- Rewriting data-heavy reports that list facts without explaining implications
- Structuring product launch announcements or case studies
- Preparing presentations where hook, problem, and call-to-action must be clearly separated
- Writing SCQA narrative for a strategy document, quarterly review, or proposal

## When NOT To Use

- Purely operational documents (runbooks, incident postmortems, API docs) — narrative adds no value
- Legal or compliance documents where completeness and precision override persuasive flow
- Internal technical specs read by developers who need depth, not narrative arc
- High-context cultures (Japan, Korea) where "answer first" structure may read as presumptuous — adapt framing

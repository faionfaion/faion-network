# Stakeholder Communication

## Summary

Stakeholder communication selects a dialogue mode before any conversation: Interview (requirements gathering), Brainstorm (option generation), Clarification (resolving ambiguity), Validation (confirming understanding), or Socratic (probing assumptions). Selecting the wrong mode — e.g., using Socratic questions during a validation session — produces incoherent conversations and erodes trust. Stakeholder mapping by power/interest determines communication frequency and channel per quadrant.

## Why

Different stakeholders have different communication needs: a high-power/low-interest executive needs a 2-minute summary; a high-interest subject-matter expert needs detailed clarification sessions. Using the wrong approach leads to missed requirements, resistance, and wasted meetings. Validation summaries sent within 24 hours of a meeting prevent silent requirement drift — the most expensive failure mode in any project.

## When To Use

- Preparing interview guides for discovery sessions (requirements gathering, user research, product feedback)
- Mapping stakeholders by power/interest before a project kickoff
- Drafting post-meeting validation summaries that stakeholders must confirm before work proceeds
- Generating Socratic question sequences to probe vague requirements like "make it faster" or "improve UX"
- Handling difficult stakeholder types (silent, dominant, negative, absent, changing)

## When NOT To Use

- Replacing live dialogue — agent-generated questions are preparation aids, not substitutes for real conversations
- Situations with high political sensitivity where each word carries organizational weight beyond the stated content
- Legal or contractual negotiations requiring specific protocol
- Power/Interest grid placement without a human who knows the organizational context — job titles alone misplace politically influential stakeholders

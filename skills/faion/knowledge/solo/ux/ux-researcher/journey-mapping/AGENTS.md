# Customer Journey Mapping

## Summary

A visualization of the complete end-to-end experience a specific persona has with a product or
service across all stages, actions, touchpoints, thoughts, emotions, pain points, and
opportunities. Current-state maps diagnose problems; future-state maps set a design target.
Must be grounded in research (interviews, analytics, support data) — not assumption.

## Why

Optimizing individual touchpoints without seeing the full journey creates local maxima: one
screen improves while handoffs between channels or departments get worse. Journey maps force
cross-functional visibility into the complete experience, surface friction that no single team
owns, and give stakeholders a shared reference for prioritizing improvements by impact on the
overall experience rather than by departmental ownership.

## When To Use

- Before a major redesign — understand the current state before designing the future state.
- When handoffs between channels or departments cause user drop-off.
- In a cross-functional workshop — the map artifact aligns teams with different domain views.
- When analytics show drop-off at a stage but the cause is unclear — mapping reveals the
  surrounding context that quantitative data misses.

## When NOT To Use

- Without prior research — a map built from assumptions is a fiction document, not a research
  output. Conduct at least 5 user interviews before mapping.
- When the scope is a single screen or component — journey mapping is for multi-step,
  multi-touchpoint experiences; use a task analysis or usability test for isolated flows.
- As a one-time deliverable — maps become stale; plan to update after major product or policy
  changes.

# Usability Testing

## Summary

A structured method for observing real users attempt predefined tasks with a product. Sessions
measure effectiveness (task completion), efficiency (time on task), and satisfaction (post-task
ratings). Findings are prioritized by severity (Critical/High/Medium/Low) and reported with direct
recommendations. Minimum 5 participants per segment for pattern detection.

## Why

Products built on assumptions consistently have usability problems that only emerge under real
use. Usability testing catches critical issues before launch, when fixes are cheap, and produces
evidence-based severity ratings that force prioritization. Five users typically reveal 85% of
major problems (Nielsen, 1993) — making it high-value even on a limited budget.

## When To Use

- Before a major feature launch — validate that task flows work for real users.
- When conversion or activation metrics are low with no clear analytics explanation.
- When redesigning navigation or an information architecture — test with think-aloud protocol.
- After implementing a competitor's pattern — verify it works for your users, not just theirs.
- When stakeholders disagree on a design direction — let test results arbitrate.

## When NOT To Use

- When you need statistical significance across hundreds of users — use unmoderated studies
  with larger samples or quantitative A/B testing instead.
- When the question is "what should we build?" — use generative research (interviews, diary
  studies) before usability testing.
- On a prototype fidelity too low to support realistic tasks — results reflect prototype gaps,
  not product usability.
